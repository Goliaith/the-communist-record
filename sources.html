Sources & References

This site draws on the following major works and resources. All claims should be verified against the original sources.

Key Historical Works

Primary & Ideological Sources

  • Karl Marx & Friedrich Engels — The Communist Manifesto (1848), various works on historical materialism
  • Vladimir Lenin — What Is to Be Done? (1902), “The State and Revolution,” writings on the party, terror, imperialism, and democratic centralism
  • Joseph Stalin — “Foundations of Leninism,” speeches on collectivization and purges
  • Mao Zedong — “On Contradiction,” “On Practice,” “Little Red Book” selections
  • Antonio Gramsci — Prison Notebooks (cultural hegemony, long march through institutions)
  • Frankfurt School authors (Max Horkheimer, Theodor Adorno, Herbert Marcuse) — Critical Theory texts, including Marcuse on “repressive tolerance”
  • Ludwig von Mises — “Economic Calculation in the Socialist Commonwealth” (1920)
  • Friedrich Hayek — The Road to Serfdom, writings on the knowledge problem and central planning
  • Aleksandr Solzhenitsyn — The Gulag Archipelago

Modern Analysis

  • Heritage Foundation reports on cultural Marxism and institutional influence
  • Works tracing the influence of Critical Theory in education and policy
  • Democratic Socialists of America (DSA) official platform and program documents: https://www.dsausa.org/about-us/what-is-democratic-socialism/ and https://platform.dsausa.org/
  • DSA caucus statements: Red Star Caucus (https://redstarcaucus.org/points-of-unity/), Communist Caucus (https://communistcaucus.com/), Marxist Unity Group

  • Additional: Books and reports on 21st-century examples (e.g., Venezuela's collapse, China's surveillance state, North Korean defector accounts)

Economic and Scientific Comparisons

  • Angus Maddison / Maddison Project Database: Historical GDP per capita and growth rates (pre- and post-reform China, East/West Germany comparisons).
  • World Bank data: GDP per capita trends, extreme poverty reduction under market reforms.
  • Nobel Prize records: Distribution by country and economic system (Nobel Foundation archives).
  • Our World in Data / historical economic studies: Long-term comparisons of living standards, innovation metrics.
  • Ludwig von Mises and Friedrich Hayek: Foundational texts on calculation problem and incentives (as cited in why-it-fails section).

This list is not exhaustive. Readers are encouraged to consult the original texts and multiple historians.
